Developing Town: Preston Passionate for CV Pennant

by Claudia Erickson Citizen Correspondent

The Cache Valley Pennant was an item to be prized by the teams playing in that crazy year when Preston citizens of all ages and both genders were “nuts about baseball.” Feelings ran high and the editors of the local newspapers added fuel to the fire with their sports editorials from the south end of Cache Valley in Logan to the north with Preston’s diatribe. In the August 5, 1909 edition of the Preston News a headline read “Preston Robbed of Pennant“ and the following article stated facts (as seen from this side of the fence.) “The enmity of Cache Valley towns is plainly seen in the action taken ….A coterie of partisan baseball directors got together and by pooling their interests, literally robbed Preston of the Cache Valley Pennant. All year Preston has played the crowds, the umpires and policemen and never uttered a word of protest and even won a great majority of the games than any other team, but when it came to the final decision, Logan, Lewiston and Richmond got together and voted that Richmond should have it.” . . . A week later there were comments that the publishers of the Logan Journal were not happy with Preston’s perspective of the past proceedings. As things go, the Preston paper followed Logan’s complaints with this: “We have only been asking for fair play and if our language is more forcible than polite, it has been called for by petty jealousy and kindergarten pique displayed by some baseball enthusiasts in this valley.” Preston’s editor, Watkin L. Roe, a man of many words, always seemed able to give as good as he got. The members of the Preston League Team of 1909 were a mixture. Not all hailed from Preston. From a picture that Newell Hart received he identified possible players as Bill Haworth, Phil Margetts, Henry Head, a Keller from Mink Creek, Bert Harrison, Bob Tippets and Miles Parker. Elmer Head was the mascot. Miles Parker was the team’s manager and was employed as the railroad agent in Preston. Two out of town team members were the pitcher, surname of Smith, and the catcher Red Settes. Information on these players is scarce, some not at all. Phil Margetts was a very active part of the development of this area it seems, a business man, active in theatricals, a musician with evidence of playing both drums and violin. He also had a touring troupe that provided programs at the Opera House and other communities. Teams from the north end of Cache Valley that have appeared in the research of that time are ones from Fairview, Mound Valley and Dayton. It is sensible to assume the other villages had teams, since the sport was popular throughout this area. Most of the last names of those players that are identified in existing pictures are still prevalent in our county in the 21st Century. Again, these were players of the game, but lived full lives in their own communities as businessmen, farmers, active in their chosen church affiliations. Baseball was a part of everyday life for young and old. The Franklin County Citizen of April 1912, writes that the Oneida Academy baseball squad was busy practicing on the campus. Ward histories from all over Franklin County speak of the various communities celebrating Memorial Day, July 4, Pioneer Day on July 24. They all had a huge feast, pot luck style, games for the younger ones and the big event – a baseball game for entertainment. Village against village. Most of these players were only interested amateurs, who got together on their Saturday afternoons to relax and play some ball. However, they were dedicated to the sport and many were very good players. The competition provided just as much enthusiasm and spirit as did those games that were played for the Cache Valley Pennant.
